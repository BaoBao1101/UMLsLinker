A 43-year-old nonsmoker male, a waiter and father of 2 healthy children, with an unremarkable past medical history, was referred to our neurosurgery department via the emergency room in January 2025. He presented with rapidly developing heaviness and numbness in both lower limbs, occurring over a period of less than 48 hours. This presentation was in the context of apyrexia and without any history of trauma or evidence of infection. The patient reported that this heaviness was preceded by transient episodes of mechanical lower back pain, which had been evolving over several months. The pain became more pronounced when sitting or standing for prolonged periods, consistent with his work requirement of standing for over 10 hours daily. He also noted that his lower back pain was accompanied by a pulling sensation radiating from the left gluteal region to the anterior aspect of his thigh, just above the knee, along with a sensation of coldness in his entire left lower limb. He additionally complained of recent voiding difficulties, but denied other associated sexual dysfunctions, reporting a maintained physiological morning erection. His brother reported no improvement in symptoms despite an intravenous injection of glucocorticoids administered by a private physician the day before his emergency room visit. Upon presentation, the patient was in a wheelchair, and transferring him to the examination table was very difficult due to significant pain. Examination revealed that pain was aggravated by the slightest lumbar flexion and extension, both in passive and active range of motion. The Kemp test was positive on the left side, and passive elevation of the right leg was positive at 70°, though it was almost impossible on the left due to pain. Muscle assessment confirmed more pronounced paraparesis on the left, graded as 4/5, with abolition of the left patellar osteotendinous reflex. There was marked hypoesthesia affecting almost the entire left lower limb, without any saddle anesthesia. The patient presented with a bladder globe, but bone palpation revealed no joint restriction at other levels or at the sacroiliac joints. The remainder of the somatic examination showed no abnormalities, and the patient's hemodynamic state was stable. Laboratory tests, including complete blood count, C-reactive protein, and blood electrolyte levels, were all within normal ranges. The erythrocyte sedimentation rate was also normal. Based on these findings, we primarily considered a lumbar disc herniation compressing the cauda equina, particularly at the L3-L4 level. However, a synovial cyst, spinal hematoma, or even a cauda equina tumor were also part of our differential diagnosis. To better support the diagnosis, an emergency gadolinium-enhanced lumbar spine MRI (Fig. 1) was performed. This revealed an intradural and extramedullary lumbar lesion lateralized to the left at the L2-L3 level, exhibiting isosignal intensity on both T1 and T2-weighted sequences, with no significant enhancement following gadolinium chelate injection. The lesion compressed and displaced the Dural sheath to the right, measuring 15 × 8 mm in the axial plane and 24 mm in height. At the L3-L4, L4-L5, and L5-S1 levels, disc bulges were observed in contact with the Dural sheath, associated with posterior interapophyseal osteoarthritis. The conus medullaris was located at the L1 vertebra and appeared normal. The electromyography (EMG) and nerve conduction velocity (NCV) test revealed signs of peripheral neurogenic damage with significant axonal loss, particularly notable in the L5 roots. The motor conduction velocities of the right and left common fibular (external popliteal) and tibial (internal popliteal) nerves, as well as their distal motor and sensory latencies, were normal. Similarly, the study of the sensory conduction velocities of the right and left sural and superficial fibular (musculocutaneous) nerves showed no abnormalities. These findings excluded truncal neuropathy or polyradiculoneuritis, instead suggesting a partial cauda equina syndrome (CES). Our patient received a short-term systemic bolus of corticosteroids to achieve a quicker and stronger anti-inflammatory effect, combined with intravenous paracetamol infusion. The diagnosis was communicated to him, and informed consent was obtained to schedule surgery. All his laboratory findings remained within normal limits. In the operating room, the patient was anesthetized and intubated, with the endotracheal tube secured while he remained in the supine position. He was then carefully turned to a prone position, with parallel rolled sheets and blankets placed longitudinally under his chest, lateral to the breasts, to relieve abdominal pressure. The patient was secured to the operating table with a belt across his thighs, just below the buttocks. A 4-6 cm incision was made, and a surgical approach was performed, involving muscle dissection. Once the lumbar canal was accessed, the L2-L3 level was identified using a mobile C-arm fluoroscopic X-ray machine. A flavectomy with L2 and L3 laminectomy, along with bilateral partial facet resection, were performed. Upon opening the vertebral canal, a migrating whitish extradural disc herniation was discovered. This herniation was covering and compressing the entire left lateral side of the Dural sheath, extending to its posterior aspect. The herniated disc was soft and very easy to extract without the need for traction by a disc clamp (Fig. 2). Disc curettage continued until satisfactory relaxation of the Dural sheath was achieved. Closure was performed in the classic manner, with a suction Redon drainage system in place. Histopathological study of the surgical specimen (Fig. 3) revealed the presence of hyaline fibrocartilage containing numerous clusters of chondrocytes, along with tears and clefts. The fibrocartilage matrix exhibited myxoid and granular areas. The chondrocytes, which were slightly hyperplastic, had small, dark nuclei and occasional prominent nucleoli. Several clusters of these chondrocytes were surrounded by a basophilic matrix, and the chromatin appeared more open. Vessels with a branched architecture were often observed, with scattered spindle-shaped fibroblastic cells and lymphocytes present throughout the cartilage and between the vessels. There was no significant associated cytological atypia. Overall, these findings were consistent with an intervertebral disc exhibiting degenerative changes.Fig. 3Histopathological features of the surgical specimen. On the fifth postoperative day, the patient was discharged after his back was immobilized with a semi-rigid lumbar support belt made from inelastic, 3-layer, breathable fabric. He was then referred to the department of physical medicine and rehabilitation for further care. At his 15-day follow-up appointment at our outpatient clinic, the patient exhibited no residual weakness or hypoesthesia in his left lower limb.
