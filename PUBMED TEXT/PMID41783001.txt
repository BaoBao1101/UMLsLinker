The patient is a 32-year-old woman with a significant occupational history involving work as a factory sewing machine operator, a job that necessitates maintaining a static sitting posture for over 8 h per day. She is G2P2 (Gravida 2, Para 2), with an unremarkable past medical and surgical history. She denies current or past use of tobacco or alcohol. The woman presented for neurosurgical evaluation following referral for the progressive management of chronic, debilitating low back pain. This pain, which was of insidious onset and had increased in severity, is explicitly described as non-radicular, lacking the typical dermatomal distribution consistent with classic sciatica or lower extremity radiculopathy. Critically, the pain was mechanically induced, becoming markedly exacerbated by even minimal axial spinal loading, and had demonstrated therapeutic resistance to conventional pharmacological interventions, specifically non-opioid analgesics and non-steroidal anti-iflammatory drugs. Further historical inquiry reveals an absence of crucial "red flag" symptoms, including a denial of sphincter or sexual dysfunction, neurogenic claudication, antecedent spinal trauma, or recent febrile episodes. Upon physical examination, the woman demonstrated mild to moderate antalgic posturing and was mildly distressed. Active and passive range of motion of the thoracolumbar spine was significantly restricted, manifesting as a severe limitation in spinal mobility. This restriction was compounded by pronounced hypertonia and guarding of the paravertebral musculature upon palpation. Quantitative assessment revealed severely impaired forward flexion, specifically noted by a finger-to-floor distance exceeding 1 m and a pathological result on the Schober Test. Importantly, a detailed neurological assessment of the lower extremities yielded no evidence of focal motor deficit, with osteotendinous reflexes symmetrically intact and bilaterally preserved. Furthermore, the patient exhibited no signs of sensory deficit in the lower limbs, nor was there any indication of saddle anesthesia, further mitigating suspicion of a significant central or cauda equina compressive lesion. The lumbar computed tomography (CT) scan (Fig. 1) exhibited a significant intracanal lesion in the lumbar spine, which is described as isodense and dimensioned at 14 × 53 mm (anteroposterior × craniocaudal). This lesion is strategically positioned to occupy and expand the epidural space between the L2 and L3 vertebral levels. Crucially, the absence of associated osseous erosion or bony involvement at the adjacent vertebral bodies or posterior elements suggests that the lesion is primarily soft-tissue in origin rather than an aggressive osseous tumor. The subsequent lumbar MRI (Fig. 2) was instrumental in the definitive characterization of the lesion, confirming an expansive mass situated within the posterior epidural canal at the L2-L3 intervertebral level, now dimensioned at 17 mm in thickness by 56 mm in height. This mass exhibited a characteristic T1-weighted hyposignal and a T2-weighted hypersignal semiology, with a heterogeneous signal on the Short Tau Inversion Recovery (STIR) sequence. Crucially for diagnosis, the lesion demonstrated marked and homogeneous enhancement following the intravenous administration of gadolinium chelate, Furthermore, this pathological process exhibited bilateral foraminal extension at L2-L3 and exerted significant mass effect, resulting in the anterior displacement and severe compression of the cauda equina nerve roots. All these feature suggested a highly vascular or cellular tumor such as an epidural neurogenic tumor (e.g., schwannoma) or a highly cellular metastatic process. The strong suspicion of a highly vascular lesion, derived from the marked and homogeneous enhancement observed on the post-gadolinium T1-weighted MRI sequence, naturally necessitates the inclusion of hemangioma in the differential diagnosis. Given the lesion's substantial size and the associated severe mass effect leading to significant anterior displacement and compression of the cauda equina roots, the immediate clinical imperative remains rapid surgical decompression and subsequent histopathological analysis to definitively rule out more aggressive, though less common, epidural tumors enhancement. The preoperative electroneuromyography study yielded normal physiological data throughout, effectively ruling out a concurrent peripheral neuropathy or established chronic radiculopathy. Specifically, motor and sensory conduction velocities and distal latencies were preserved in the bilateral lower limb nerves. Crucially, the needle showed a complete absence of pathological activity at rest (e.g., fibrillation potentials), thereby excluding acute or subacute denervation in the tested L2-S1 musculature. During maximal contraction, motor unit potentials exhibited normal morphology and recruitment. All laboratory investigations were within normal limits. The complete blood count showed a leukocyte count of 6500 cells/mcL, RBC of 4 million cells/mcL, hemoglobin of 13 g/dL, hematocrit of 39%, and platelets of 240,000 cells/mcL. The electrolyte panel was unremarkable: sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 4.5 mEq/L, chloride 101 mEq/L, calcium 8.6 mg/dL, and magnesium 2.3 mg/dL. The hemostasis panel revealed a prothrombin time of 13 seconds, an international normalized ratio of 0.94, and an activated partial thromboplastin time of 25 seconds. Her chest X-ray and her electrocardiogram demonstrated no acute cardiopulmonary abnormalities. Written informed consent was obtained from the patient during pre-assessment clinics and surgery was scheduled. Following the induction of general anesthesia and orotracheal intubation, the patient was carefully positioned in the prone position on the operating table, utilizing plain dyed medical cotton rolls beneath the chest and iliac crests; this maneuver was crucial for minimizing intra-abdominal pressure to optimize venous return and consequently reduce peroperative bleeding. The surgical access started with a standard posterior midline incision, precisely delineated from L1 to L4, followed by a meticulous subperiosteal dissection and bilateral detachment of the paraspinal musculature to expose the posterior spinal elements. Subsequently, a bilateral L2-L3 laminectomy was executed, performed in conjunction with a meticulous flavectomy, achieving comprehensive and safe decompression and exposure of the underlying dural sac. Intraoperatively, the ligamentum flavum exhibited pathological calcification at the L2-L3 segment. Upon dural exposure (Fig. 4), a distinct epidural mass was visualized, manifesting as a compact, intensely dark red to purplish-red mass. Macroscopically, the tissue was characterized by a gelatinous/globular consistency, possessing a smooth, glistening, and irregularly lobulated or scalloped surface, consistent with a highly vascular or potentially mucoid/hemorrhagic pathological entity such as hematoma or angiolipoma. The lesion was noted to occupy the entire posterior aspect of the dural sac. Crucially, a clear surgical cleavage plane existed between the lesion and the underlying dura mater, facilitating its easy mobilization and blunt dissection. No significant compressive mass effect on the dural sac was observed, nor was there evidence of direct invasion into adjacent structures. The entire specimen was ultimately resected en bloc without complication and without the use of the ultrasonic aspiration. The excised specimen (Fig. 5), characterized by its irregular, amorphous shape and lobulated margins, was visually likened to the morphology of a duckbill eagle ray (Aetobatus spp.). The surgical bed was smoothly and meticulously irrigated, and hemostasis was performed prior to closure. The immediate postoperative course was clinically uneventful. Specifically, the woman reported a significant and marked improvement in her lower back pain, a benefit that was sustained even during periods of orthostasis and when ambulating with the aid of an adjustable lumbar support orthosis. The histopathological analysis and microscopic examination (Fig. 6) revealed a well-circumscribed mass surrounded by a thin fibrous capsule. The tumor is composed of a benign proliferation featuring two distinct intermingled components, often arranged in lobules separated by delicate fibrovascular or collagenous septa. The prominent vascular component consists of numerous thin-walled capillary structures lined by regular endothelial cells, with lumina dilated and engorged with erythrocytes. Interspersed throughout is the adipocytic component, composed of regular, mature adipocytes. Crucially, there is a complete absence of histological features of malignancy, including necrosis, cytological atypia, nuclear pleomorphism, or atypical mitotic activity. The patient was deemed medically stable and subsequently discharged on day 4 postoperatively. The prescribed discharge regimen included non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs and analgesics for optimized pain management. A structured follow-up appointment at our outpatient neurosurgical clinic was scheduled to monitor long-term recovery, assess wound healing, and evaluate the sustained neurological and symptomatic improvement. During the scheduled 30-day postoperative outpatient appointment, our patient reported the complete and sustained resolution of her pain. Her symptomatic improvement was sufficiently robust to permit the discontinuation of the lumbar support belt as early as postoperative day 10. Clinical assessment, including a detailed neurological examination, yielded entirely unremarkable findings. Local inspection of the surgical site confirmed the incision was clean, fully consolidated, and in excellent cosmetic condition. At the three-month outpatient appointment, the woman reported being completely asymptomatic. She confirmed that she had successfully and gradually discontinued the use of the lumbar immobilization and support belt and had fully resumed her professional activities without any restrictions or pain.
