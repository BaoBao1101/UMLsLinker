A 29-year-old patient presented with cauda equina syndrome characterized by saddle numbness, urinary incontinence, and bilateral foot drop. She underwent an urgent decompression for a disc herniation, at which time the surgeons encountered severe OLF, causing cauda equina compression. After performing a computed tomography scan to assess the extent of ossification, the patient was taken back to the operating room for completion of the original laminectomy with extension to an adjacent level. Her postoperative recovery was uneventful. A 29-year-old female presented with cauda equina syndrome. Over a short period, she developed severe bilateral leg pain, progressive saddle numbness, urinary incontinence, and bilateral foot drop. An urgent magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) lumbar spine picked up cauda equina compression at the L4-5 level due to central disc herniation with degenerative dorsal facet hypertrophy [Figure 1]. However, intraoperatively, an ossified midline lesion was encountered dorsal to the theca. Consideration was given to extending the incision and fenestrating the canal at an adjacent segment, but OLF is commonly multi-segmental, and it was unclear how many segments of the patient’s lumbar spine were involved. Drilling was aborted because of concerns of iatrogenic injury to the cauda equina. When the STAT computed tomography (CT) scan [Figure 2] demonstrated the OLF at L4-5, the patient was taken back for decompression at L3-4 and L4-5, along with an L4-5 discectomy. Within 2 postoperative months, the bilateral footdrop improved, along with her bladder function. Note hyper-intensity at the dorsal aspect of theca on axial images depicted by the arrow. Computed tomography with arrow demonstrating ossification of the ligamentum flavum at L4-5 level.
