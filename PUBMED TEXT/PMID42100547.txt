We report a case in which an enterogenous cyst located ventral to the thoracic spinal cord was completely excised surgically without aspirating the cyst fluid. In addition, a comprehensive literature review and analysis are presented. The Institutional Review Board of Beijing Tsinghua Changgung Hospital approved this study. The necessary patient informed consent was obtained in this study. A 57-year-old woman presented with 1 month of progressive bilateral leg weakness, numbness, pain, and urinary dysfunction. On admission, she had an indwelling catheter. Left lower limb muscle strength was grade 1 in proximal and distal muscle groups; right side was grade 2. Hypotonia, hyperreflexia, and bilateral Babinski signs were present. MRI showed a ventral subdural lesion at T9-T10 with hypointense T1-weighted imaging and hyperintense T2-weighted imaging signals, matching cerebrospinal fluid (Figure 1A-1C). Axial imaging revealed the “spinal cord embedding sign,” suggesting an enterogenous cyst. Preoperative tests (electrocardiogram, chest x-ray, blood count, liver and kidney function) showed no surgical contraindications. The lesion was resected by a posterior midline approach. To prevent cyst fluid leakage and aseptic meningitis, the cyst was removed intact without aspiration. After general anesthesia and prone positioning, a midline incision was made at T9-T10. The surgical procedure (Figure 2A-2F) proceeded as follows: An ultrasonic bone scalpel was used for partial laminectomy. Hemostasis was achieved with gelatin matrix and bipolar coagulation. Under microscopy, the dura was opened and suspended. The cyst—grayish-white, soft, encapsulated, and well-defined—was found ventral to and embedded within the spinal cord, with firm adhesions. Microsurgical dissection separated it from neural tissue. The intramedullary base was carefully freed. En bloc resection was completed. After hemostasis, the dural defect was repaired with synthetic graft and anti-adhesion patch. The lamina was reconstructed with titanium plates. A drainage tube was placed extradurally. Layers were closed. Postoperatively, she received mannitol, methylprednisolone, and antibiotics. One week later, MRI confirmed complete resection with no residual or recurrent lesion (Figure 3A-3C). Histopathology confirmed enterogenous cyst (Figure 4A and 4B). Two weeks postsurgery, leg strength remained unchanged (grade 2), but bladder and bowel function improved. She started hyperbaric oxygen therapy and passive rehab. Active strengthening began at 1 month. At 3 months, right leg strength reached grade 4, left grade 5. She walked independently with crutches.
