A 44-year-old Japanese man was transferred to our emergency department with limb weakness. His medical history included Graves' disease diagnosed and treated 14 years prior; however, he repeatedly discontinued treatment on his own. He had not been receiving treatment for Graves' disease the year prior to his presentation. On the day of admission, he received an injection containing 3.3 mg dexamethasone (equivalent to 22 mg prednisolone) at another clinic to block the lumbar nerve root and alleviate buttock pain caused by lumbar herniation. Several hours later, he experienced weakness in his lower limbs, which progressively worsened to a point where he had difficulty walking. He denied a recent history of strenuous exercise, binge eating, diarrhea, infection, trauma, or diuretic use. At the time of admission, the patient was taking diclofenac sodium, tramadol hydrochloride, and mirogabain besylate for pain related to lumbar herniation. His family history was not significant, including periodic paralysis, and he consumed alcohol only on social occasions. Three years prior, the patient had developed transient paralysis following an injection of dexamethasone at a trigger point in the buttocks while self-discontinuing treatment for Graves' disease. At that time, the test results were as follows: thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH), ＜0.01 mIU/L; free triiodothyronine, 4.56 pg/mL; free thyroxine, 2.35 ng/dL; thyroid stimulating hormone receptor antibody (TRAb), 2.23 IU/L (reference: ＜2.0); anti-thyroglobulin antibody (TgAb), 395.0 IU/mL (reference: ＜40.0); anti-thyroid peroxidase antibody (anti-TPO Ab), 536 IU/mL (reference: ＜28.0); radioactive iodine uptake value, 31.2% (reference: 10-40%). The patient was diagnosed with relapse of Graves' disease, and thiamazole treatment was resumed. On admission, a physical examination revealed a body temperature of 36.6°C, pulse rate of 103/min, blood pressure of 164/104 mmHg, respiratory rate of 16 breaths/min, and oxygen saturation of 98% on ambient air. The patient was alert and conscious. Exophthalmos was not observed, and the thyroid gland was neither enlarged nor tender. A manual muscle test revealed the following results: bilateral biceps, triceps, flexor carpi radialis, extensor carpi radialis 4/5, bilateral hamstring 4/5, bilateral iliopsoas, and quadriceps muscle 3/5. Sensory testing revealed intact responses and deep tendon reflexes in the limbs were normal. A laboratory examination revealed a white blood cell count of 7,000/μL, a hemoglobin level of 16.8 g/dL, a platelet count of 22.0×104/μL, creatine kinase level of 110 U/L, urea nitrogen level of 8.7 mg/dL, creatinine level of 0.65 mg/dL, sodium level of 142 mEq/L, potassium level of 2.5 mEq/L, magnesium level of 1.9 mg/dL, calcium level of 10.1 mg/dL, glucose level of 159 mg/dL, C-reactive protein level of 0.71 mg/dL, TSH level of ＜0.01 mIU/L, free triiodothyronine level of 4.56 pg/mL, and free thyroxine level of 2.35 ng/dL (Table 1). The patient was diagnosed with TPP related to dexamethasone injection into the lumbar nerve root. Laboratory Findings on Admission. AST: aspartate aminotransferase, ALT: alanine aminotransferase The patient was treated with infusions of 20 mEq of potassium chloride administered in a 500 mL solution with a potassium chloride concentration of 40 mEq/L over 2 h, three times every 3 h. His limb weakness resolved when his serum potassium level reached 2.9 mEq/L. In addition, he was administered 26.8 mEq of oral potassium preparations. After 24 h of admission, his serum potassium level increased to 4.1 mEq/L. The patient was discharged 48 h after admission (Figure) and continued treatment for Graves' disease at another hospital where he had previously attended. The hospital reported to us that the patient's test results were as follows: TRAb, 3.71 IU/L; anti-TgAb, 311.0 IU/mL; anti-TPO Ab, ＞600 IU/mL; technetium-99m uptake, 2.99% (reference: 0.5-3.5%). The estimated thyroid weight on thyroid ultrasound was 41.1 (reference: 15-20) g. They determined that the patient had experienced a flare-up of Graves' disease and resumed treatment with thiamazole.
