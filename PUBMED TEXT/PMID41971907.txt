We report the case of a 67-year-old woman with advanced osteoporosis who presented with severe low back pain, neurogenic claudication, and progressive lower limb weakness. Radiographs and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) revealed degenerative lumbar scoliosis, multilevel vacuum disc phenomena, Meyerding grade I–II spondylolistheses, and severe foraminal stenosis from L2 to S1. After preoperative optimization with teriparatide therapy, a two-stage surgical strategy was implemented. The first stage involved a single-access, four-level OLIF (L2–S1) using the sliding window technique to restore disc height and alignment. The second stage consisted of percutaneous pedicle screw fixation from L2 to S1 with polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) cement augmentation to enhance screw stability in the osteoporotic vertebrae. Both stages were completed without complications. The patient experienced significant postoperative improvement, including pain reduction and restoration of independent ambulation. The study was reviewed and approved by the Ethics Committee of the National Center for Neurosurgery (Astana, Kazakhstan) (No. 7). A 67-year-old female patient presented to the Department of Spinal Neurosurgery and Peripheral Nervous System Pathology at the National Center for Neurosurgery with complaints of severe lower back pain, numbness in both lower extremities, and progressive lower limb weakness. Her symptoms had been present for over 7 years and had progressively worsened in the preceding 2–3 months, particularly in terms of motor function. During this time, she had undergone multiple courses of conservative treatment without sustained clinical benefit. Her medical history included a lumbar discectomy at the L4–L5 level more than a decade earlier. Anthropometric assessment revealed obesity, with a height of 149 cm, a weight of 95 kg, and a BMI of 42.8 kg/m2. Comorbid conditions included arterial hypertension and type 2 diabetes mellitus. Neurological examination revealed significant lumbar pain rated 8–9 on the Visual Analog Scale (VAS), accompanied by paravertebral muscle spasm. The pain was aggravated by changes in posture and axial loading and was refractory to non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) and analgesics, including ketoprofen, metamizole sodium, ibuprofen, and diclofenac. Percussion and palpation of the lumbar spine elicited marked tenderness. Deep tendon reflexes were symmetrically diminished (D=S), and motor strength in the lower limbs was graded 4/5. A mild paraparesis was noted. The Oswestry Disability Index (ODI) score was 70%, indicating severe functional impairment. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and computed tomography (CT) of the lumbosacral spine were performed. CT revealed marked vacuum disc phenomena at L2–L3, L3–L4, L4–L5, and L5–S1, consistent with advanced disc degeneration. Degenerative lumbar scoliosis was noted, with a Cobb angle of 25° between L2 and L5, along with prominent lateral osteophyte formation. Multilevel degenerative spondylolisthesis was observed: Meyerding grade II at L4–L5 and grade I at L3–L4. Segmental disc space collapse was evident at multiple levels. Dynamic flexion-extension radiographs demonstrated segmental instability from L1 to L5, with translational movement exceeding 5 mm. Preoperative CT was also used to quantify bone quality by measuring Hounsfield units (HU) at the instrumented levels. The measured HU values at the lumbar vertebral bodies scheduled for fusion were all below 100 HU, consistent with severe osteoporosis, which further supported the decision to use cement-augmented pedicle screw fixation. After confirmation of severe osteoporosis on CT-based HU measurements, teriparatide therapy was initiated as soon as the indication for surgery was established. Because of the patient’s progressive pain, neurogenic claudication, and mechanical instability, surgery could not be delayed for a prolonged preoperative course. Therefore, teriparatide was administered for approximately 1 month before surgery and was continued postoperatively as part of long-term osteoporosis management, with the aim of initiating anabolic bone therapy rather than achieving complete structural reversal within the preoperative period. MRI showed abnormal lumbar alignment with disruption of both sagittal and coronal balance. Findings included reduced intervertebral disc height, multilevel foraminal stenosis, and instability involving motion segments from L2 to S1. CT, computed tomography. Given the patient’s osteoporotic background, a comprehensive bone health assessment was performed. Laboratory investigations confirmed adequate serum vitamin D levels. The patient’s fracture risk assessment tool (FRAX) score indicated a high risk for osteoporotic fragility fractures. Following a multidisciplinary team discussion, the patient was initiated on teriparatide therapy (a recombinant parathyroid hormone analog) 1 month prior to surgery. This decision aimed to improve bone mineral density and enhance the mechanical stability of spinal instrumentation. The use of anabolic therapy in this context is supported by current evidence, which demonstrates improved outcomes and reduced complications in spinal fusion procedures among patients with severe osteoporosis (9). The case was reviewed in a multidisciplinary spine conference involving spinal neurosurgeons and musculoskeletal radiologists. Consensus was reached that the patient’s clinical presentation, combined with radiographic evidence of progressive deformity and multilevel instability, rendered further conservative management unlikely to succeed. Given the severity of neurogenic symptoms, spinal canal stenosis, and coronal/sagittal imbalance, surgical intervention was indicated to achieve indirect neural decompression and long-segment stabilization. The key surgical challenges included compromised bone quality due to advanced osteoporosis and the need to address pathology across multiple spinal segments. The primary objective was to ensure robust and durable spinal fixation to prevent implant loosening, migration, or construct failure. A two-stage surgical approach was planned. The first stage involved multilevel OLIF via a single mini-open oblique lateral approach using the sliding window technique. The second stage consisted of percutaneous pedicle screw fixation with polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) cement augmentation to enhance screw purchase and overall construct stability in osteoporotic bone. To mitigate the risks associated with osteoporosis, the surgical plan incorporated cement-augmented pedicle screws, a strategy supported by biomechanical evidence demonstrating enhanced initial pull-out strength and reduced mechanical complications in osteoporotic vertebrae. After obtaining written informed consent, a two-stage surgical procedure was performed. In the first stage, the patient was positioned in the right lateral decubitus position. A single left-sided mini-lumbotomy was carried out through a 74-mm skin incision using the Triple-Arm retractor system. Under fluoroscopic (C-arm) guidance, sequential microdiscectomies were performed at the L2–L3, L3–L4, L4–L5, and L5–S1 levels. Interbody cages were inserted using the sliding window technique, enabling access to four levels through one oblique retroperitoneal corridor. Disc preparation was performed in a conservative manner, limited to removal of cartilaginous tissue and loose disc material with ring curettes under fluoroscopic guidance, while preserving the subchondral endplates. Wide-footprint OLIF cages spanning the apophyseal ring were used and gently impacted to restore disc and foraminal height without excessive distraction, thereby reducing the risk of endplate violation and vertebral endplate fractures in the setting of severe osteoporosis. The goals of the first stage—restoration of disc height, realignment of spinal curvature, and indirect decompression of neural structures—were successfully achieved. Following completion of the anterior stage, the patient was repositioned into the prone position on a radiolucent operating table, and the second stage was performed within the same operative session. A minimally invasive percutaneous pedicle screw fixation with vertebral cement augmentation was carried out to provide additional mechanical stability in the osteoporotic vertebrae. Under intraoperative fluoroscopic guidance, Jamshidi needles were inserted bilaterally with a 45-degree oblique trajectory through the pedicles of L2, L3, L4, L5, and S1. The needles were advanced toward the mid-body region of each vertebra. Once satisfactory placement was confirmed, the stylets were removed, and vertebroplasty was performed at all five levels. A total of 8 mL of PMMA bone cement was injected into the vertebral bodies under real-time fluoroscopic monitoring. Cement distribution was homogeneous and confined within the vertebral bodies, with no evidence of leakage or extravasation. Subsequently, 10 self-tapping polyaxial pedicle screws were inserted percutaneously into the instrumented levels. Two pre-contoured titanium rods were mounted and secured using locking caps. The construct achieved sagittal and coronal realignment with restoration of spinal balance. The posterior construct is stable, and multilevel interbody fusion (four cages) was achieved through a single incision with minimal muscular disruption. The total operative time was approximately 6 hours, including both stages, with an estimated intraoperative blood loss of 200 mL. No intraoperative complications were noted. The patient was extubated in the operating room and transferred to the recovery unit in stable condition. The patient was transferred to the recovery unit approximately 2 hours after completion of the procedure. Postoperative pain management was initiated with intramuscular ketoprofen (100 mg), administered twice daily during the first 4 postoperative days to control incisional and musculoskeletal pain. As the patient’s condition improved, the frequency of analgesic administration decreased. By postoperative day 4, pain intensity had declined from 8–9 to 6–7 points on the VAS, with improved tolerance to mobilization. Early mobilization was initiated according to a standardized protocol: verticalization with assistance was achieved on postoperative day 3, followed by progressive ambulation under supervision. A semi-rigid thoracolumbosacral orthosis (TLSO) was prescribed and continuously used during mobilization and daily activities for 3 months to provide additional spinal stability and reduce the risk of implant-related complications. Neurological examination showed preserved motor strength in the lower extremities at 4/5, consistent with the preoperative baseline. The patient reported significant subjective improvement in axial back pain and noted reduced discomfort during early ambulation and verticalization. Glycemic control was closely monitored due to the patient’s history of type 2 diabetes mellitus. At admission, her glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) was 7.32%. Blood glucose levels remained within acceptable limits (≤8.0 mmol/L) throughout the postoperative period, without the need for insulin therapy adjustments. Postoperative CT confirmed appropriate positioning of all interbody cages and pedicle screws.
