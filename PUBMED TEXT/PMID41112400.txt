A 63-year-old male with chronic back pain experienced sudden exacerbation and progressive bilateral lower limb weakness. Magnetic resonance imaging with contrast revealed a sequestrated L2/3 disc and adjacent epidural hematoma compressing the thecal sac. Minimally invasive decompression and hematoma evacuation were performed. A ruptured epidural vein was identified intraoperatively. Neurological improvement was immediate, and the patient was discharged on postoperative day 2. A 63-year-old male with a history of chronic lower back pain reported sudden worsening of pain 7 days before admission. He developed bilateral lower limb weakness, saddle hypoesthesia, and urinary hesitancy approximately 12 h before presenting to the emergency department. Neurological examination revealed reduced power bilaterally. On the right: hip flexion 3/5, knee extension 2/5, ankle dorsiflexion 4/5, extensor hallucis longus 5/5, and plantar flexion 5/5. On the left: hip flexion 3/5, knee extension 3/5, and ankle dorsiflexion and plantar flexion 5/5. No coagulopathy or anticoagulant use was identified. Lumbar spine magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) with contrast revealed a large L2/3 sequestrated disc herniation and adjacent posterior epidural hematoma compressing the thecal sac and cauda equina. This is illustrated in Figure 1, which shows axial and sagittal T2-weighted MRI images demonstrating the hematoma and disc fragment. Contrast imaging excluded mimics such as abscesses, vascular malformations, or metastasis. (a) Sagittal T2-weighted MRI showing L2/3 disc herniation (red arrow), adjacent posterior epidural hematoma (green arrow), and posterior displacement of the thecal sac (blue arrow). A cross yellow line indicates the axial section level. (b) Sagittal short-tau inversion recovery (STIR) image demonstrating hyperintense signal consistent with hematoma (green arrow), with a cross yellow line marking the axial cut level. (c) Axial T2-weighted MRI confirming left paramedian disc extrusion (red arrow) compressing neural elements and the thecal sac (blue arrow). An urgent minimally invasive decompression and hematoma evacuation were performed using a tubular retractor. Intraoperative findings included a prominent epidural vein near the disc fragment, likely the hemorrhagic source. Figure 2 depicts the intraoperative view, highlighting the epidural vein adjacent to the sequestrated disc. Intraoperative microscopic view showing the dura, sequestrated disc fragment, and ruptured epidural vessel–source of hemorrhage. On the 1st postoperative day, motor strength improved significantly. On the right side, knee extension was 4/5, and all other myotomes were 5/5. On the left side, knee extension was 4+/5, hip flexion 4/5, and the remaining myotomes were 5/5. He was discharged on postoperative day 2 with near-complete neurological recovery.
