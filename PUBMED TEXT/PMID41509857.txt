We present the case of a 20-year-old female who reported a 6-month history of progressive low back pain and severe left-sided L5 radiculopathy. Imaging revealed a nidus in the left L5 pedicle consistent with OO. The lesion was successfully resected using a full-endoscopic pedicle-based approach with the RIWOspine system. The procedure was performed under fluoroscopic guidance, allowing direct visualization and excision of the nidus. The patient experienced immediate symptom relief, and postoperative imaging confirmed complete resection. Histopathological examination confirmed the diagnosis of OO. At 1-year follow-up, she remained asymptomatic with no evidence of recurrence or spinal instability. A 20-year-old female without underlying disease presented with a 6-month history of progressive low back pain accompanied by severe radiculopathy radiating to the left lower limb in the L5 distribution. The pain was worse at night and partially relieved by NSAIDs. Neurological examination was unremarkable except for a positive straight leg raise test on the left side. Plain radiographs were inconclusive. Computed tomography (CT) revealed a well-defined radiolucent nidus with surrounding sclerosis located in the left L5 pedicle (Figure 1A-1B). Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) demonstrated adjacent bone marrow edema and without impingement on the left L5 nerve root (Figure 1C,1D). A diagnosis of spinal OO was made. A full-endoscopic resection via a pedicle-based approach was performed using the RIWOspine endoscopic system (RIWOspine GmbH, Knittlingen, Germany). Under general anesthesia, the patient was positioned prone, and a pedicle-based working channel was established under biplanar fluoroscopic guidance. After cortical bone drilling, the nidus was directly visualized and excised under continuous endoscopic irrigation. The nidus appeared as a hypervascular reddish lesion embedded within sclerotic bone and was resected completely using a high-speed burr and endoscopic graspers (Figure 2A-2H). Fluoroscopic images confirmed proper trajectory and instrument positioning throughout the procedure (Figure 3A-3H). No intraoperative complications occurred. The patient reported immediate resolution of radicular symptoms and was discharged on the second postoperative day. She resumed normal activities within 2 weeks. Histopathological analysis confirmed the diagnosis of OO, revealing interlacing trabeculae of woven bone rimmed by osteoblasts embedded within a fibrovascular stroma (Figure 4A-4C). At the 3-month and 1-year follow-ups, the patient remained asymptomatic without recurrence of pain or neurologic symptoms. Follow-up CT and MRI confirmed complete nidus removal, resolution of bone marrow edema, and preservation of spinal stability (Figure 5A-5D).
