Here we report the first case of transient unilateral sixth nerve palsy following general anesthesia for a non-ocular surgical procedure (adenoidectomy) in a 5-year-old child. Ocular motility assessment confirmed right-sided abducens nerve palsy and MRI excluded underlying pathology. The right eye movement gradually improved over the following 2 weeks and the child made a full recovery. A 5-year-old boy with no significant past medical history underwent a routine adenoidectomy under general anesthesia. The surgery proceeded without complications, and the child was extubated. However, within a few hours of the operation, he developed right-sided diplopia and could not abduct the right eye. On examination, he had normal visual acuity in both eyes. Lateral movement was limited in the right eye, but movements were normal in the left eye. There was no evidence of ptosis or other ocular abnormalities. Neurological examination was otherwise unremarkable, and the other cranial nerves showed no abnormalities. Given the sudden onset of symptoms, the child was thoroughly assessed. Ocular motility assessment confirmed right-sided abducens nerve palsy and a full range of motion in other directions. MRI of the brain and orbits was normal, ruling out ischemic event, intracranial pathology, and structural abnormalities. The child was treated with supportive measures, focusing on symptomatic relief and reassurance with regular monitoring at follow-up visits over the following days. Over the course of 2 weeks, the child’s right eye movement gradually improved, and full recovery was observed without residual symptoms at follow-up at 2 weeks.
