We report a 10-month-old male infant with a 7-month history of a progressively enlarging midline lumbosacral mass. The swelling, measuring 15 × 13 cm, was soft, non-tender, and had ill-defined margins. Initial ultrasonography was inconclusive. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the spine showed a heterogeneous subcutaneous lesion without spinal cord involvement. Complete excision biopsy was performed, and histopathology confirmed the diagnosis of FHI, showing the characteristic triphasic pattern of mature adipose tissue, fibrocollagenous stroma, and immature mesenchymal elements. The postoperative course was uneventful, and the patient remains well at 2 months of follow-up with no evidence of recurrence. This is a case of a 10-month-old male infant who was referred from a peripheral facility, about 86 km from our tertiary center, for further evaluation and management on account of a progressive midline lumbosacral swelling. The lesion was not observed at birth. The mother first noticed the mass at 3 months of age as a small lump that gradually doubled in size between 7 months and 10 months. It was painless, with no ulceration, discharge, or associated systemic symptoms. The child’s general growth parameters remained within normal limits. He achieved neck control at 3 months and rolled over at 4 months. By 6 months, he could sit with support; however, as the mass enlarged, his mother observed difficulty maintaining an upright sitting posture. He began crawling at 9 months. Antenatal history was unremarkable. The mother initiated antenatal care at 16 weeks of gestation and was compliant with folic acid (400 µg daily) and prenatal vitamins. Routine fetal anomaly scans were normal. The child was delivered at 40 weeks by emergency cesarean section due to prolonged labor, with an incident-free neonatal course. No family history of similar lesions or congenital anomalies was reported. The child was first presented at 7 months of age with a mass measuring approximately 7 × 8 cm and was in a stable condition. However, the treatment was delayed due to difficulty in affording the diagnostic workup and definitive treatment, as well as the unavailability of a neonatal intensive care unit bed required for postoperative care. On presentation at 10 months, the infant was alert, with a Pediatric Glasgow Coma Scale score of 15/15. Vital signs and anthropometric parameters were age-appropriate. Head-and-neck examination revealed no abnormality, and the anterior fontanelle admitted a fingertip. Neurological assessment showed normal tone and reflexes in all limbs. Gross and fine motor skills, language, and social milestones were age-appropriate. Examination of the back revealed a 15 × 13 cm ovoid, lumbosacral midline mass, approximately 1 cm above the gluteal cleft [Figure 1]. The overlying skin showed hyperpigmentation with no ulceration, dimpling, or discharge. The mass was firm, non-tender, minimally mobile, and not adherent to the overlying skin. Abdominal, cardiovascular, and respiratory examinations were unremarkable. The overlying skin appears intact with faint hyperpigmentation and no ulceration, punctum, or discharge. Ultrasonography (USG) of the lesion showed a heterogeneous soft-tissue mass with irregular margins, suggestive of lipoma or inflammatory myositis. A lumbosacral spine MRI performed at 8 months of age revealed normal vertebral alignment and preserved conus medullaris terminating at L1, with no evidence of dysraphism or neural involvement. A subcutaneous mass measuring 2.86 × 8.29 × 6.43 cm was visualized posterior to the sacral spine. The underlying bones were intact, and there was no communication with the spinal canal [Figure 2]. Sagittal view of T2-weighted magnetic resonance imaging of the lumbosacral region showing a heterogeneous subcutaneous mass posterior to the sacral spine (arrow A). The lesion appears predominantly hyperintense with central areas of hypointensity, with no communication to the spinal canal or evidence of dysraphism. The conus medullaris (arrow B) is at the level of L1. Based on these findings, the child underwent an excisional biopsy under general anesthesia. Intraoperatively, the lesion was poorly defined but well encapsulated by fibrofatty tissue, with no attachment to underlying muscle or fascia. The postoperative period was uneventful. Histopathological report showed a spindle cell tumor arranged in fascicles, interspersed with islands of primitive myxoid mesenchyme with an intimate lipomatous component. The spindle cell regions comprised collagenous and fibrous stroma. In addition, angiomatoid collagenized areas were observed in close association, along with pockets of mature fat lobules. Immunohistochemical staining showed smooth muscle actin (SMA) positivity within the spindle cell bundles, vascular structures, and mesenchymal regions, and S-100 positivity confined to the adipocytic areas, confirming the diagnosis of FHI. Surgical excision achieved clear margins, with a 2 cm margin free of tumor involvement. At 2 months’ follow-up, satisfactory healing had taken place [Figure 3].
