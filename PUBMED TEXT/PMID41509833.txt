A 41-year-old woman diagnosed with MFS presented with progressive mid-thoracic and low back pain spanning over two decades. Imaging revealed characteristic findings of MFS, including severe leftward thoracolumbar kyphotic scoliosis, dural ectasia, hypoplastic pedicles (1 mm), and vertebral body scalloping. Due to debilitating pain and impaired function, a two-stage thoracolumbar fusion was planned. The 1st stage involved posterior fusion from T10 to S1, along with a planned L2 pedicle subtraction osteotomy (PSO), guided by RNA. RNA enabled accurate placement of juxtapedicular, cortical-bone-trajectory (CBT), and bilateral S2 alar-iliac (S2AI) screws, accommodating the patient’s severely hypoplastic pedicles while ensuring adequate fixation. The 2nd stage occurred one month postoperatively and involved a L3-L5 lateral lumbar interbody fusion (LLIF) and a L5-S1 anterior lumbar interbody fusion (ALIF), also guided by RNA. All procedures performed in this study were in accordance with the ethical standards of the institutional and/or national research committee(s) and with the Declaration of Helsinki and its subsequent amendments. Written informed consent was obtained from the patient for the publication of this case report and accompanying images. A 41-year-old female with MFS presented with progressive thoracolumbar kyphotic scoliosis and non-radiating low back pain spanning 20 years. Recent exacerbation of symptoms included pain at rest and nocturnal awakening due to discomfort. Given the patient’s MFS diagnosis, a heightened awareness of potential complications during spinal surgery was warranted, notably concerning her dural ectasia and stable thoracic aortic aneurysm. Physical examination revealed a left-sided thoracolumbar deformity with slight truncal asymmetry, compensated by hip and knee flexion. Radiographic imaging demonstrated a thoracolumbar scoliosis with a Cobb angle of 47 degrees and thoracolumbar kyphosis measuring 60 degrees, along with excessive compensatory lumbar lordosis. Additional findings included severe leftward convex curvature, dural ectasia, posterior vertebral body scalloping, pectus excavatum, and advanced degenerative disc disease along the concavity of the curve and at L4–L5 and L5–S1 (Figure 1). Given the patient’s severe thoracolumbar kyphosis, excessive lumbar lordosis, rigid deformity, and the structural challenges associated with MFS (including dural ectasia and hypoplastic pedicles), a two-stage procedure was planned. A PSO at L2 was specifically selected to achieve segmental shortening and sagittal realignment, as posterior fusion alone would not have allowed sufficient angular correction in the setting of a rigid deformity and compromised posterior elements. The second stage involved an RNA-guided L3–L5 lateral lumbar interbody fusion (LLIF) and L5–S1 anterior lumbar interbody fusion (ALIF). A two-stage approach was planned preoperatively, with posterior-first sequencing selected to prioritize correction of the severe, rigid thoracolumbar kyphosis through a PSO and to accommodate anticipated limitations in posterior fixation due to MFS-associated anatomical features. It was anticipated that intraoperative findings during the posterior stage would guide the precise extent and levels of anterior column support required in stage 2. Staging the procedures also served to reduce cumulative operative time and physiologic burden, as combining extensive posterior osteotomy, long-segment instrumentation, and anterior reconstruction into a single-stage procedure would have posed great operative risk. The patient was positioned prone on a Jackson table and draped in a sterile fashion. The robotic arm was positioned to maintain physical attachment to the patient bed for placement of the right posterior superior iliac spine reference pin (Figure 2). This connection was essential for verifying instrumentation during RNA registration. Intraoperative fluoroscopic imaging was obtained over the reference array and colocalized with the software planning template derived from the patient’s preoperative CT scan (Figure 3A). A midline skin incision was made, and the spine was exposed from the transverse processes of T10 to the sacral region. The RNA platform illustrated variable anatomy for each lumbar vertebra, highlighting the challenges for instrumentation (Figure 3B-3F). The surgeon templated several areas with severely hypoplastic pedicles, including pedicles at L1, L2, L3, and L4. RNA enabled individualized planning of each screw based on the specific anatomy of each vertebra. Intraoperatively, if the pedicle was absent or extremely hypoplastic, juxtapedicular screws (16) were used; if the pedicle allowed safe engagement of cortical bone, cortical-bone-trajectory (CBT) screws (17) were placed; and if the pedicle corridor was small but permitted partial purchase, in-out-in screw techniques (18,19) were employed (Figure 3B-3E). At L4, the absence of reference points and the severely hypoplastic nature of the pedicles led to the decision to abstain from instrumentation in this motion segment to mitigate the risk of neural injury (Figure 3). Bilateral instrumentation of the L3 pedicle was achieved utilizing the in-out-in technique. Finally, instrumentation of the lumbar spine ending in L2 was completed utilizing juxtapedicular screws. The surgeon used the RNA platform to identify vertebral body scalloping at S1 (Figure 3F). Initially, instrumentation was planned to start at S1; however, due to the absence of posterior bone in the S1 unilateral segments, traditional instrumentation in this region was deemed impractical. Alternatively, after lumbar instrumentation, the surgeon used RNA to plan and place bilateral S2 alar-iliac (S2AI) screws, providing stable pelvic fixation despite sacral body scalloping. After positioning all lumbosacral pedicle screws, focus shifted towards the thoracolumbar region. A spinous process clamp was applied from T11 to L1, and separate RNA registration was conducted using lateral fluoroscopy in concordance with the preoperative CT scan. Subsequently, bilateral instrumentation of the left T10–L1 pedicles was performed. Considering the patient’s Cobb angle, thoracic kyphosis, and lumbar lordosis, PSO of the L2 vertebrae, including rod and cage placement, was carried out utilizing RNA to plan the alignment correction (Figure 3). A bilateral construct consisting of a single rod, with a satellite rod planned from the left L1 to L3 screws, was created. Given the patient’s dural ectasia, the pedicles were smaller than the 9-inch Frazier tool (3 mm tip), and their cortical nature rendered conventional cancellous bone removal unfeasible (Figure 4). RNA aided in isolating the pedicles adjacent to the dural ectasia. Consequently, the pedicle was excised using Kerrisons rongeurs, and decortication of the vertebral body was performed, resulting in a significant reduction of thoracolumbar kyphosis. A substantial portion of the L2 vertebra was removed to achieve the desired correction. The satellite rod was affixed to the L1 and L3 poly-axial screw heads to complete the PSO. A 3-mm Frazier suction tip is placed adjacent to the pedicle to highlight the hypoplasia. A titanium cage, filled with autograft from the osteotomy, was inserted through the L2 pedicle site into the remaining vertebral body, followed by controlled hinge mechanism reduction for coronal and sagittal alignment reconstruction. This process allowed for cantilever reduction. Rods were secured, supplemented by offset connectors, extending from the S1 screw on the right, L5 screw on the left, and pelvic screws bilaterally. This construct contained the previously placed pedicle screws from T10–L1 but bypassed the satellite rod on the left. Set screws were applied and additional reduction by compression of the thoracolumbar deformity was performed. Local autograft and allograft containing recombinant human bone morphogenetic protein 2 (INFUSE® Bone Graft, Medtronic Spinal and Biologics, Memphis, TN, USA) were packed across interlaminar regions, transverse processes, and facet joints from T10 to the sacral region. Final tightening of all set screws was completed, followed by hemostasis confirmation. The procedure’s estimated blood loss (EBL) was 1,900 mL. Perioperative neuro-monitoring remained at baseline, indicating normal neurologic functioning, as no intraoperative complications occurred. The patient was discharged home after seven days with no postoperative complications or neurological symptoms. At the one-month follow-up visit, the patient expressed satisfaction with the initial postoperative outcomes. Daily ambulation was maintained, and the level of pain experienced was deemed appropriate, considering the extent of the procedure. One month after primary RNA-guided T10-pelvis thoracolumbar fusion including L2 PSO the patient underwent a planned, RNA-guided LLIF at L3–L5 and an ALIF at L5–S1 for additional anterior column support. During the LLIF, endplates at L3–L5 were excised, and the entire disc was removed, followed by the insertion of a cage packed with allograft containing recombinant human bone morphogenetic protein 2 (INFUSE® Bone Graft). The L5–S1 ALIF involved dissection along the iliac vascular bundle’s medial border, endplate cartilage removal, and insertion of an appropriately sized implant packed with allograft. Six months after the second stage of surgery, the patient reported decreased pain compared to preoperative levels, improved ability to stand and walk, and evidence of partial fusion on radiographic imaging (Figure 6). Postoperative EOS radiographs at 6-month postoperative follow-up in (A) AP and (B) lateral views. AP, anterior-posterior; EOS, slot-scanning radiography. Three years postoperatively, the patient reported significant improvement in both functionality and pain. She described her typical pain level as 1/10, with a maximum of 3/10 at its worst. Radiographic imaging confirmed intact hardware, stable fixation, and evidence of successful fusion (Figure 7).
