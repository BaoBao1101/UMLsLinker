The patient, a 32-year-old male with no significant prior medical history aside from seizures, presented to the emergency department 2 hours after suffering a minor head injury. His trauma occurred following a backward fall, during which he struck the occipital region of his head. Upon arrival, the patient was unconscious, exhibiting a Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) score of 7. His pupils were equal and reactive, and both his hemodynamic and respiratory parameters were stable. A sutured occipital scalp laceration was noted. A cranial CT scan performed 2 hours post-injury (Fig. 1) revealed the presence of an acute subdural hematoma (ASDH) in the left fronto-parieto-occipital region, with a midline shift of 5 mm. Notably, no fractures or parenchymal contusions were identified. Given the patient’s known seizure history, the initial depressed level of consciousness (GCS 7) was considered potentially disproportionate to the radiological findings and compatible with a postictal state. Importantly, he had equal and reactive pupils, no signs of impending herniation, and stable hemodynamic and respiratory parameters, with no parenchymal contusions or skull fractures on the initial CT. The midline shift was 5 mm (borderline), and the mixed-density aspect raised early diagnostic uncertainty (acute SDH versus hematohygroma). In this context, we selected an initial conservative strategy with strict neurological monitoring and a low threshold for surgical escalation in case of deterioration [8,9]. The rapid clinical improvement within 2 hours (GCS 13) supported the appropriateness of this cautious, closely supervised non-operative approach. On the second day post-admission, a control CT scan was performed (Fig. 2), which demonstrated near-complete resolution of the cranial subdural hematoma. Only a small residual collection was noted, and the midline shift had significantly decreased. Given the unusually rapid decrease, this early change was interpreted as redistribution/migration and/or dilution of the subdural blood with CSF, rather than true complete resorption within 48 hours. At this stage, a hematohygroma/subdural effusion remained a plausible alternative explanation, and the diagnosis was therefore interpreted with caution. However, on the fifth day, the clinical course took a new turn. The patient developed motor deficits localized to the right L5 and S1 dermatomes, manifesting as weakness and reduced sensation. A neurological examination revealed symptoms consistent with cauda equina syndrome. An urgent MRI of the lumbar spine was performed, which revealed the presence of a subdural mass extending from L3 to S2. The mass appeared hyperintense on T1-weighted imaging and hypointense on T2-weighted imaging, with no significant enhancement following the administration of contrast. These imaging findings suggested that the previously identified cranial subdural hematoma had migrated to the lumbar region, though the possibility of a spinal tumor, such as a meningioma or myxopapillary ependymoma, could not be entirely excluded at this stage [11,12] (Fig. 3).Fig. 3Sagittal MRI of the lumbar spine demonstrates a subdural mass extending from L3 to S2, showing hyperintensity on T1-weighted sequences (A) (white arrowheads) and hypointensity on T2-weighted sequences (B) (yellow arrowheads), without significant post-contrast enhancement (C) (blue arrowheads).Fig 3 – dummy alt text Sagittal MRI of the lumbar spine demonstrates a subdural mass extending from L3 to S2, showing hyperintensity on T1-weighted sequences (A) (white arrowheads) and hypointensity on T2-weighted sequences (B) (yellow arrowheads), without significant post-contrast enhancement (C) (blue arrowheads). A lumbar puncture was performed at the L5-S1 level, yielding 2 mL of thick, blood-tinged fluid, consistent with a subdural hematoma. Following this procedure, the patient’s symptoms rapidly improved, with the motor deficits resolving almost completely [13]. On the seventh day, a follow-up MRI of the lumbar spine (Fig. 4) revealed complete resolution of the subdural hematoma. The second control cranial CT scan, performed earlier on the second day, had already demonstrated the near-complete disappearance of the cranial subdural hematoma. By the time of discharge, the patient had recovered fully and displayed no residual deficits.
