A 46-year-old female patient presented with double-level disc herniations at the L4-L5 and L5-S1 segments. Following the failure of two conventional physiotherapy regimens, surgical intervention was advised as the sole treatment option. The patient, however, declined invasive surgery and opted for an intensive six-month program predominantly based on NSSD therapy. Magnetic resonance imaging scans taken after the initial onset of symptoms, prior to and two months after initiating the program, and one month following its completion, revealed significant improvements across all monitored parameters, including canal anteroposterior (AP)1 length and area, disc AP length and area, and herniation index. These structural changes indicated an almost complete restoration of the canal surface and substantial reduction of herniation at both affected levels. A forty-five-year-old female patient, with a normal body mass index and no regular engagement in sports activities, presented with significant lower back pain and functional limitations. She initially received a conventional treatment regimen comprising ten sessions of physio-kinesiotherapy, diathermy, massage, and pharmacotherapy, which yielded only minimal improvement. MRI conducted in October 2022 identified double-level disc herniation at the L4-L5 and L5-S1 segments. Specifically, the L4-L5 herniation was located in the left paramedian region, sufficiently protruding to indent the ventral surface of the dural sac and make slight contact with the L5 nerve root. At the L5-S1 level, a substantial herniated disc in the left lateral position was observed, impinging on the ventral surface of the dural sac, partially obstructing the adjacent foraminal recess, and compressing the S1 nerve root on the same side. Upon exacerbation of symptoms, the patient sought further medical intervention in February 2023. She was subsequently prescribed a rehabilitation program encompassing ten sessions of dry needling, traditional magnetotherapy, and Tecar therapy. This treatment failed to produce significant improvement, and the patient was advised to seek neurosurgical consultation. By the end of March 2023, surgical intervention was recommended as the definitive solution. However, the patient declined surgical intervention and instead pursued alternative non-surgical options at the private clinic. In April 2023, a functional examination including assessments such as the FNTT1, SLRT1, evaluation of the ability to walk on tiptoes and heels and MRC1 scale for muscle strength, confirmed substantial herniation, prompting a recommendation for a new MRI scan following neurosurgical consultation. The subsequent MRI confirmed the presence of herniated discs at the L4-L5 and L5-S1 levels, with protrusions causing compression of the adjacent foraminal recess and impingement on the L5 and S1 nerve roots. Due to the extent of the double-level herniation, an intensive non-invasive treatment regimen was promptly initiated. The treatment protocol began with five weekly sessions of combined NSSD and conventional physiotherapy over one month. In subsequent months, the treatment frequency and session content were adjusted according to the patient's clinical response. The patient underwent a total of 92 treatment sessions incorporating various therapeutic modalities. During NSSD therapy (BTL Industries, Ltd.), the patient was positioned supine with legs extended and angled downward, enabling decompression forces to target the affected spinal segments (Fig. 1). The decompression force was precisely controlled and did not exceed 50 % of the patient's body weight. Each active therapy session lasted 30 min, followed by a stabilization phase in a stationary position. Conventional physiotherapy involved various modalities (BTL Industries Ltd.) delivering different types of stimulation energies, aimed at symptom alleviation, acceleration of tissue regeneration, and facilitation of structural changes. The final month included therapeutic exercises designed to stabilize and maintain treatment outcomes.Fig. 1Patient positioning: patient position during NSSD treatment.Fig. 1 Patient positioning: patient position during NSSD treatment. Follow-up MRI scans conducted two months into the treatment and one month post-treatment completion revealed notable structural changes, most prominently in the sagittal plane, and quantitatively assessed improvements from axial images. Despite the visible positive changes, the MRI data were further processed to provide a quantitative evaluation of structural changes, offering a comprehensive view of the patient's condition progression (MicroDicom DICOM Viewer 2023.3 × 86). The HI1 was calculated using the equation published by Choi et al. [18]:HI=HL×HWTCL×TCW×1000where:HL = Herniation Length determined by the maximum anteroposterior length (AP) of the disc herniation,HW = Herniation Width, determined as the width of the disc at the level of half of the HL distance,TCL = Total Canal Length determined as the maximal AP canal length,TCW = Total Canal Width determined as the width of the canal at the level of half of the HL distance. In addition to these indicators, the parameters herniation area, canal area, and canal length were also assessed. In this context, canal length refers to the distance from the lowest point of the disc to the lowest point of the canal [19]. Values obtained from MRI image processing since the initial symptom manifestation in October 2022 demonstrate structural changes throughout the entire monitored period (Table 2). The MRI taken before the commencement of the treatment program confirmed significant deterioration in the patient's condition between October 2022 and April 2023, particularly at the L4-L5 vertebral level. Two months after initiating treatment, a slight improvement was observed, progressing to near-complete recovery at both spinal segments. In terms of herniation AP length, improvements exceeded 5 mm and 4 mm in the L4-L5 and L5-S1 segments, respectively, representing over 73 % improvement at both levels.
