An 89-year-old woman with atrial fibrillation, chronic heart failure, and hypertension presented to our cardiology department for the evaluation of severe aortic valve stenosis and underwent ultrasound and computed tomography (CT). She required urgent care. The patient suddenly collapsed during lunch in the hospital cafeteria. An emergency call was made at the hospital. Responding medical personnel determined that the patient was in cardiac arrest, initiated chest compressions in accordance with the standard method, placed her on a gurney, and transported her to the emergency room (ER). Five minutes after securing an intravenous line, intubation of the trachea, and two rounds of adrenaline in the ER, the patient regained spontaneous circulation. A large amount of food was aspirated from the trachea. Chest compressions were performed for approximately 15 minutes. A CT scan showed kyphosis, DISH, an extension fracture (reverse chance fracture) of the second lumbar vertebra, and a large right Gerota's intrafascial hematoma. The patient died on the same day. No autopsy was performed.
