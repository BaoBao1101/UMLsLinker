A 29-year-old Javanese male was diagnosed with GBM. Following gross total tumor resection, he underwent a full course of adjuvant radiotherapy and chemotherapy. The posttreatment period was initially uneventful, with no apparent adverse effects. However, 4 months after completion of therapy, the patient experienced a sudden onset of progressive quadriparesis, accompanied by urinary and bowel dysfunction. Contrast-enhanced cranial magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) revealed no evidence of tumor recurrence. Subsequently, a contrast-enhanced spinal MRI demonstrated extensive intradural extramedullary metastases spanning from the cervical to the lumbar spinal segments. Histopathological analysis of a biopsy specimen confirmed the spinal lesions as metastatic GBM. Given the extent of disease and associated risks, the patient and his family declined invasive therapeutic interventions. Supportive and palliative measures were provided, and the patient ultimately succumbed to respiratory failure shortly thereafter. A 29-year-old Javanese man presented to the Mochtar Riady Comprehensive Cancer Center, Siloam Hospital Semanggi Jakarta, neurosurgery clinic with progressive weakness affecting all four limbs. Thirteen months earlier, he had been diagnosed with a right temporal lobe tumor following a contrast-enhanced magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the head. Subsequently, a right temporal craniotomy and gross total resection were performed. Histopathological examination confirmed the diagnosis of GBM, WHO Grade IV. Postoperatively, the patient underwent concomitant chemoradiotherapy, including external beam radiotherapy with a total dose of 60 Gray (Gy) delivered in 30 fractions over 6 weeks, alongside oral temozolomide at 75 mg/m2 daily during radiotherapy. This was followed by 6 cycles of adjuvant temozolomide at 150–200 mg/m2/day for 5 days every 28 days, per the hospital protocol. Following treatment completion, the patient reported resolution of acute neurological symptoms and remained stable until approximately 3 weeks before the current presentation. On examination at the clinic, the patient was mobilizing with crutches. Neurological examination revealed Medical Research Council (MRC) grade 3/5 paraparesis, increased muscle tone, and hyperreflexia in both lower extremities, without clonus or Babinski sign. There was no motor or sensory deficit in the upper extremities. Sensory examination showed a sensory level at T10 bilaterally. Cognitive examination was within normal limits. Differential diagnosis included spinal cord pathology due to primary or metastatic disease. Considering the history of GBM, recurrent or metastatic disease was suspected. A repeat contrast-enhanced MRI of the brain was requested. MRI of the brain showed no evidence of local recurrence or progression. Postoperative gliotic changes and cerebromalacia communicating with the right lateral ventricle were identified [Figure 1], but these findings were not sufficient to explain the current neurological deterioration. The patient was advised to return for regular weekly follow-up. However, he did not attend subsequent appointments. Serial had magnetic resonance imaging with contrast (a) before chemotherapy and (b) after the sixth session of chemotherapy. Three months later, the patient returned in a wheelchair, with worsening symptoms including progressive quadriparesis over 3 weeks and urinary retention onset the night prior. Neurological examination revealed MRC grade 2/5 weakness in all extremities, sensory loss with a sensory level at T6, and suprapubic tenderness. Deep tendon reflexes were brisk in all limbs, and there was absent voluntary anal contraction on rectal examination. Vital signs were stable with blood pressure 138/80 mmHg and heart rate 80 bpm. Due to clinical deterioration, the patient was admitted for further investigation and supportive care. A contrast-enhanced MRI of the entire spine revealed a homogeneous, contrast-enhancing intradural extramedullary mass extending continuously from the cervical to the lumbosacral spine, with associated spinal cord compression [Figure 2]. A lumbar puncture was performed; cytological analysis of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) showed no malignant cells. (a) Spinal MRI with contrast shown contrast enhancing homogeneous mass in the vertebral canal from the cervical to the lumbosacral level leads to consequential spinal cord compression. With laminectomy, (b) the tumor was exposed and (c) dissected. Given the patient’s clinical status, radiologic findings, and previous GBM diagnosis, spinal metastasis of GBM was suspected. Treatment options, including decompressive laminectomy and tumor debulking, were discussed. However, the patient and family declined extensive surgical intervention due to the associated risks. They consented instead to a limited laminectomy with biopsy for diagnostic purposes. The procedure was performed 2 days after admission. Postoperatively, the patient was monitored in the intensive care unit for 3 days and transferred to the general ward for 2 additional days before discharge. Two weeks later, the histopathological examination revealed an GBM tissue characteristics [Figure 3]. Weekly follow-up at the outpatient clinic was advised. Given the irreversible neurological deficit, the absence of viable therapeutic endpoints, and patient preference, palliative management was initiated. A multidisciplinary supportive care plan was proposed, including psychological counseling, home care services, and symptom management, but these were also declined. The patient died of respiratory failure approximately 3 months after the last admission.
