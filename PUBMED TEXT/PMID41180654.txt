A 62-year-old woman was admitted with a progressively enlarging left lumbar mass present for nearly 2 years. One and a half years earlier, she had noticed a ping-pong ball-sized, hard, smooth, and painless mass in the left waist. A computed tomography (CT) scan showed a 6.9 × 6.5 cm cystic-solid mass in the same region. The patient, from a remote rural area in China, had limited access to medical and financial resources. After the initial admission, she was treated with oral traditional Chinese medicine, which had no significant effect. She then discontinued treatment, and the mass rapidly enlarged. Nine months later, she developed fullness and pain in the left lumbar region, with skin over the mass turning purple and blood vessels becoming visible. By readmission 6 months later, the mass had grown to the size of a watermelon. The patient had no known family history of malignancy, genetic disorders, or chondrosarcoma. Her medical history included hypertension for 4 years and a cerebral hemorrhage 10 years ago that resulted in left-sided hemiplegia, which recovered with treatment. A giant left trunk mass was observed, with its upper edge below the left clavicle, the medial edge crossing the midline, the lower edge below the left anterior superior iliac spine, and the posterior edge near the midline. Enhanced MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging) revealed multiple irregular, patchy enhancements with unclear boundaries involving the left half of the T10 vertebra and left paravertebral soft tissue from T10 to L1. The lesion partially merged with the pleura and peritoneum, protruding into the abdominal cavity and displacing adjacent structures. The left ribs appeared normal, with a small left pleural effusion. Findings suggested a giant multilocular cystic-solid mass extending from the left chest wall to the lumbar region, predominantly cystic, involving the left retroperitoneal and perirenal spaces, the left half of the T10 vertebra, and the left paravertebral muscles from T10 to L1, consistent with a malignant tumor. Ultrasound revealed an indistinct spleen and a large, inhomogeneous echoic area in the left abdomen, measuring approximately 26 × 28 cm, with unclear boundaries, an irregular shape, and abundant blood flow signals. Single-photon emission CT showed abnormal radiotracer uptake in the left 9th, 10th, and 11th ribs and left iliac bone, suggesting a high likelihood of malignancy. The lesion extended from the left axilla to the left iliac region, with no distant metastasis. An ultrasound-guided percutaneous biopsy revealed atypically proliferative cartilaginous tissue with hemorrhage and extensive necrosis, suggestive of chondrosarcoma with necrosis. Cytological smears showed numerous scattered and clustered spindle cells, identified as tumor cells. Decalcified sections confirmed atypically proliferative chondroblasts, consistent with chondrosarcoma. Immunohistochemistry was positive for S-100, with a Ki-67 index of 1%, and negative for pan-cytokeratin. Molecular testing such as IDH1/2 analysis was not performed due to socioeconomic and resource limitations, and diagnosis was established based on pathology and imaging. The diagnosis was giant chondrosarcoma originating from the left T10 vertebral accessory and abdominal wall, accompanied by left pleural effusion, left lower lobe atelectasis, and pelvic effusion. After completing preoperative examinations and preparations, a multidisciplinary surgical team was assembled, including specialists in spinal, thoracic, urologic, general, hepatobiliary, and plastic surgery, along with anesthesiology. Spinal surgeons evaluated vertebral involvement and ensured spinal stability; thoracic surgeons assisted in chest wall exposure and pleural management; urologic and hepatobiliary teams protected retroperitoneal organs; general surgeons contributed to abdominal wall dissection; plastic surgeons managed soft tissue closure and reconstruction; anesthesiologists monitored hemodynamics and managed massive blood loss. The anesthesiology team implemented multiple strategies to manage anticipated massive blood loss, including preoperative cross-matching and preparation of blood products, invasive arterial and central venous pressure monitoring, intraoperative use of rapid transfusion systems and cell salvage and close monitoring of acid-base balance, urine output, and coagulation parameters to ensure hemodynamic stability throughout the procedure. Under general anesthesia, the patient was positioned on the right side with the left arm suspended. An irregular surgical incision was designed based on the shape and location of the tumor – an arc-shaped incision from the left axilla to below the left anterior superior iliac spine, with an additional incision extending over 50 cm. Intraoperatively, the tumor was extremely large and lobulated, with partial skin involvement and invasion of major muscle groups, including the latissimus dorsi, serratus anterior, and abdominal muscles. Significant destruction of the left-sided attachments of the 9th, 10th, and 11th thoracic vertebrae was observed, with the 10th thoracic segment most severely affected. The surgery began with identifying and protecting vital structures, including the left lung, spleen, left kidney, ureter, left axillary vascular and neural structures, and major retroperitoneal vessels. The tumor size severely restricted the surgical field and maneuverability, making complete en bloc resection unfeasible. Instead, the tumor was meticulously resected in segments to ensure complete removal within its peripheral margins. After complete tumor resection, the soft tissue at the surgical site was lax, requiring direct suture repair of the abdominal and chest walls. Although spinal attachments were resected, spinal stability remained intact, and internal fixation was unnecessary. Multiple posterior rib segments were removed, but intraoperative assessment indicated minimal respiratory impact and a low risk of postoperative paradoxical respiration, eliminating the need for thoracic cage reconstruction. Postoperatively, negative-pressure drainage tubes were placed in the axilla, back, thoracic cavity, and iliac fossa.
