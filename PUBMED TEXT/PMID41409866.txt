A 73-year-old woman sustained a vertebral compression fracture at L1 following a fall. Progressive vertebral collapse caused upward displacement of the conus medullaris and slackening of the spinal cord, leading to bilateral thigh pain and myelopathic symptoms. Decompression and fixation surgery were performed. Due to osteoporosis, screws were inserted through the endplates and augmented with bone cement to prevent implant failure. Postoperatively, the patient’s symptoms resolved, and her neurological function improved. A 73-year-old woman fell from a chair and suffered an L1 compression fracture. Conservative treatment was initiated, but about 10 days later, she developed electric shock-like pain in both anterior thighs upon trunk extension, which prevented her from lying supine. She maintained a flexed, lateral decubitus posture to avoid pain. Neurological examination revealed paresthesia and pain in both anterior thighs, with mild weakness of the iliopsoas and quadriceps muscles (manual muscle testing [MMT], 4/5). No sensory loss in the perineal area or bladder-rectal dysfunction was observed. MRI obtained 3 days after injury revealed retropulsion of the posterior wall of the L1 vertebral body, causing moderate canal stenosis, along with degenerative spondylolisthesis at L4/5. At 21 days post-injury, MRI demonstrated upward migration of the conus medullaris with S-shaped slackening and tortuosity of the spinal cord above the compressed segment [Figure 1]. (a) Preoperative MRI 3 days after injury showing L1 vertebral body collapse. (b) Preoperative MRI 21 days after injury showing cranial displacement of the conus medullaris and slackening of the spinal cord. A laminectomy of L1 was performed, and the ventrally protruded bony fragment was reduced by posterior compression. Pedicle screws were inserted from T12 caudally and from L2 cranially, penetrating the adjacent endplates. The screws were reinforced with polymethylmethacrylate. Hydroxyapatite was packed into the L1 vertebral body for reconstruction, and fixation was extended from T12 to L2. Postoperatively, the patient’s shock-like pain disappeared, muscle strength improved, and she regained independent ambulation. MRI confirmed restoration of normal spinal cord morphology, and sagittal alignment remained satisfactory at 1-year follow-up [Figure 2].
