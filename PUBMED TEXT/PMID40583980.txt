A 50-year-old female presented with a history of low back pain and left-sided radiculopathy along the L5 dermatome, which had been persistent for a few months. On neurological examination, her muscle power was graded as 4/5 in the left lower limb and 5/5 in the right lower limb according to the Medical Research Council (MRC) grading system. Reflexes and tone were normal in both lower limbs, and her plantar response was flexor bilaterally. No definite sensory level was detected, but the anal tone was noted to be lax. These findings were initially suggestive of a possible prolapsed lumbar intervertebral disc (PLID) at L4–L5 rather than a spinal schwannoma, which led to the consideration of a differential diagnosis. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) was performed to investigate the patient’s symptoms further. The MRI revealed an intradural extramedullary space-occupying lesion (SOL) at the L1–L2 level, which was a significant finding (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3, Fig. 4). Interestingly, the MRI also demonstrated a fluid–fluid level within the lesion. This feature is rare for spinal schwannomas, which are usually characterized by a uniform appearance on MRI. The presence of a fluid–fluid level, most commonly seen in aneurysmal bone cysts, soft-tissue cavernous hemangiomas, and simple bone cysts, in this case raised suspicion for a different pathology, although it was consistent with blood products, pointing towards the possibility of intratumoral hemorrhage. A paracentral disc herniation was also noted at the L4–L5 level on the left side, but it was considered incidental given the predominant findings at L1–L2. The fluid–fluid level on the MRI suggested the presence of cystic degeneration and hemorrhage within the tumor (Fig. 3, Fig. 4), which is an unusual but documented feature in some vestibular schwannomas, though it is exceedingly rare in spinal schwannomas. Given the radiological features, the decision was made to proceed with surgical intervention to both confirm the diagnosis and relieve the patient’s symptoms. A long midline incision was made extending from D12 to L4, and the paraspinal muscles were carefully retracted through subperiosteal dissection. Laminectomy was performed from L1 to L2, allowing for adequate exposure of the lesion. Durotomy was carried out to open the dura mater and expose the intradural tumor (Fig. 5, Fig. 6). The tumor, which appeared unusual due to its blackish color rather than the typical purplish hue associated with schwannomas, was carefully dissected away from the nerve roots. This blackish appearance was attributed to the presence of hemorrhagic material within the tumor capsule, which had been highlighted by the MRI findings. The tumor was excised en bloc with its capsule, ensuring that all neural elements were preserved, and no significant damage to surrounding structures occurred. After ensuring proper hemostasis, duraplasty and laminoplasty were performed to reconstruct the dura and stabilize the spine. Histopathological examination of the excised tumor confirmed the diagnosis of a schwannoma (Fig. 7). The tumor showed spindle cell proliferation, which is consistent with schwannomas, and revealed cystic degeneration and intratumoral hemorrhage. The hemorrhage within the tumor was consistent with the radiological findings of the fluid–fluid level observed on MRI. The tumor was encapsulated, and there was evidence of vascular congestion and mild nuclear pleomorphism in some of the cells. These histological findings supported the conclusion that the tumor was a schwannoma with retrograde changes, including cystic degeneration and hemorrhage.Fig. 7Histopathology of an encapsulated schwannoma with retrograde changes, showing cystic degeneration, mild nuclear pleomorphism, and congested blood vessels. This case is particularly noteworthy due to the rarity of spinal schwannomas presenting with fluid–fluid levels on MRI, a feature commonly associated with vestibular schwannomas. The potential mechanisms behind intratumoral hemorrhage in schwannomas include the vascular fragility of the tumor, repeated microhemorrhages leading to cystic degeneration, and increased tumor vascularity that may result in spontaneous hemorrhage. The findings of cystic degeneration and hemorrhage in this case highlight the importance of considering atypical features on MRI and correlating them with histopathological findings for accurate diagnosis and management. The initial misdiagnosis of PLID in this case emphasizes the need for careful and thorough MRI evaluation in patients presenting with low back pain and radiculopathy. When evaluating an intradural extramedullary space-occupying lesion at the L1–L2 level, the differential diagnosis must encompass a range of spinal pathologies beyond the initially suspected prolapsed lumbar intervertebral disc (PLID). In addition to aneurysmal bone cysts, soft-tissue cavernous hemangiomas, and simple bone cysts, ependymomas represent a critical consideration, particularly in the lumbar spinal canal. Ependymomas, which are more commonly intramedullary but can occasionally present as intradural extramedullary lesions, typically arise from ependymal cells lining the central canal and may exhibit cystic or hemorrhagic components on MRI, potentially mimicking the fluid–fluid level observed in this case. Unlike schwannomas, ependymomas may demonstrate a more homogeneous enhancement pattern and are often associated with a broader range of neurological deficits, including sensory and motor impairments, due to their frequent intramedullary location. In this patient, the presence of a fluid–fluid level and the lesion’s extramedullary location favored a schwannoma, but the inclusion of ependymoma in the differential diagnosis was essential to ensure a comprehensive diagnostic approach. The histopathological confirmation of schwannoma, characterized by spindle cell proliferation and cystic degeneration, ultimately distinguished it from ependymoma, underscoring the importance of correlating radiological findings with histological analysis to guide accurate diagnosis and management. The presence of a fluid–fluid level in a spinal tumor should raise suspicion for an underlying schwannoma, particularly in cases where other clinical features do not align with typical presentations. This case underscores the importance of a multidisciplinary approach in the diagnosis and management of complex spinal tumors, as well as the need for detailed imaging and histopathological correlation to ensure optimal treatment outcomes.
